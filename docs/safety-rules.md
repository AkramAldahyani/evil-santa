# Safety, Ethics, and Legal Use

Evil Twin techniques sit at the heart of how real attackers steal Wi-Fi credentials and intercept traffic. Studying them is valuable for defenders — but only within strict boundaries. This page defines those boundaries.

---

## The One Rule That Matters Most

> **Never target a network, device, or person without explicit, written authorization.**

Wireless attacks are not "harmless tests." Broadcasting a fake access point, knocking devices offline, or capturing passwords affects real people and is illegal in most jurisdictions when done without consent — even if you never misuse what you collect.

---

## Where This Knowledge Belongs

Acceptable contexts for hands-on wireless security work include:

- **Your own equipment** — networks and devices you personally own.
- **A physically isolated lab** — a closed setup with no connection to other networks or the public.
- **Authorized engagements** — penetration tests or assessments with a signed scope and written permission from the network owner.
- **Conceptual study** — reading, diagrams, and awareness training that do not involve attacking live networks.

---

## What Is Never Acceptable

Do not, under any circumstances:

- Deauthenticate or jam networks you do not own (this is a denial-of-service attack).
- Clone a real network's SSID to lure its users.
- Present fake login or "router update" pages to capture passwords from non-consenting people.
- Collect, store, or validate credentials that are not yours.
- Intercept, monitor, or modify other people's traffic.
- Operate in public places (cafés, airports, campuses) where bystanders may connect.

These actions cause real harm and carry serious legal consequences. No educational framing changes that.

---

## Legal Reality

Unauthorized wireless attacks can violate computer-misuse, wiretapping, and telecommunications laws. Penalties range from heavy fines to imprisonment. Laws vary by country, but the common thread is simple: **interfering with networks or capturing data without permission is a crime.**

If you are unsure whether something is permitted, assume it is not — and get written authorization first.

---

## A Note on Awareness Material

The most valuable thing you can do with this knowledge is help others defend themselves. Teaching people to recognize fake networks, use VPNs, and distrust password prompts protects far more people than any attack demonstration ever could.

---

## Key Takeaway

Curiosity is good. Authorization is mandatory. Use this knowledge to defend, teach, and protect — never to intrude.
